Ordered Network Analysis (ONA), my first signature method, has been employed in 100+ published works across quantitative ethnography, learning analytics, AI in education, healthcare, and policy analysis since its inception (ONA method paper). ONA was recognized as Best Student Paper at the 2022 International Conference on Quantitative Ethnography.

Epistemic Trajectory Modeling (ETM), my current ongoing project, extends ONA to model learning trajectories. Rather than summarizing learning as a static network, ETM traces the underlying mechanisms that drive behavior change over time to describe learning as a dynamic process as it actually unfolds. I also investigate adjacent topics that inform this work:

Visual design. I believe the alignment between numerical and visual representations shapes how people interpret results. I've written about the layout algorithm behind ONA networks and ENA's graphic design, with a focus on supporting faithful and intuitive reading of network analysis results.

Simulations as measurement spaces. How do you measure learning in open-ended environments where there's no single right answer? In this work, I used ONA to visualize player trajectories in a simulated space generated from iPlan, an urban planning game, by constructing a normative model of expert behavior against which learner activity can be projected and compared (Thin Data, Thick Description).
